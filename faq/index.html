👻GHOSTTOOTH

A Bluetooth scanner that helps you discover nearby devices — including potential surveillance gadgets and hidden trackers.

Help & FAQ · a labidi.eu project

GHOSTTOOTH listens for the little radio “hellos” that Bluetooth devices broadcast and shows them to you in a clear, live list — the device’s name, signal strength, an estimated distance, its maker, and when it was last seen. It’s built to help you notice things like tag-style trackers or covert recording devices in your space.

This page explains, in plain language, how it works, how to read the results, and — just as importantly — what it honestly can’t see. Bluetooth is powerful but imperfect, and an honest tool tells you where its limits are.

01 What is GHOSTTOOTH?

A Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) scanner and detector for everyday awareness.

GHOSTTOOTH is a Bluetooth (BLE) scanner/detector. It discovers nearby Bluetooth devices that are broadcasting and presents them so you can review what’s around you. That includes ordinary gadgets (earbuds, watches, TVs, sensors) as well as devices worth a second look, such as:

  • Tag-style trackers — small location tags people attach to keys, bags… or, misused, to a person or vehicle.
  • Potential surveillance devices — some hidden cameras, wearable recorders and smart glasses advertise over Bluetooth.

Think of it as a radio “who’s nearby” list. It doesn’t connect to, control, or interfere with other devices — it just listens to the public announcements they already send out.

02 How the app works, step by step

It passively listens for BLE advertisement packets and turns them into a readable list.

  1. Devices broadcast. Most BLE devices periodically send tiny radio messages called advertisement packets — a public “I’m here” beacon that any nearby receiver can hear.
  2. GHOSTTOOTH listens. When you start a scan, your phone’s Bluetooth radio passively captures those packets. No pairing, no connection, no data sent to the device.
  3. It parses each packet. The app reads the fields inside: any broadcast name, the device address, manufacturer-specific data (including a Company Identifier), advertised service UUIDs, and the signal strength (RSSI).
  4. It classifies & lists. Each device becomes a row showing name, maker, signal, an estimated distance, and a last-seen time. Known tracker/surveillance signatures are flagged.
  5. It keeps updating. As packets keep arriving, rows refresh live — signal rises and falls as devices (or you) move around.
A bit more technical detail

BLE advertising happens on three dedicated channels. Your phone “scans” by hopping across them and reporting each packet it hears, along with the RSSI measured by its radio. GHOSTTOOTH reads the advertisement (and, where present, the scan-response) payload — it does not open a GATT connection to interrogate the device. Everything shown comes from what the device already chose to broadcast.

03 Permissions & prerequisites

A few switches must be on before your phone will reveal nearby Bluetooth devices.

  • Bluetooth must be ON. The scanner uses your phone’s Bluetooth radio; if it’s off, nothing can be heard.
  • “Nearby devices” / Bluetooth scan permission. On modern Android (12+), the app asks for the Nearby devices permission to scan.
  • Location permission on older Android (6–11). These versions require Location access before they’ll return Bluetooth scan results.
Why does a Bluetooth scanner need location permission?

It’s an Android rule, not a data grab. Because Bluetooth beacons can be used to infer where you are (beacons sit in fixed places), older Android versions gate BLE scanning behind the Location permission as a privacy safeguard. GHOSTTOOTH does not use GPS or track your location — on Android 12+ it declares its scan as neverForLocation precisely to make that explicit. Granting it simply unlocks the ability to see advertisement packets.

04 Signal strength (dBm / RSSI) explained

RSSI is how loudly your phone hears a device. It’s measured in dBm and it’s always negative.

RSSI (Received Signal Strength Indicator) is reported in dBm, and the numbers are negative. The key rule:

  • Closer to 0 (e.g. −40 dBm) → stronger signal → usually closer.
  • More negative (e.g. −90 dBm) → weaker signal → usually farther.
−40 (strong / near)−100 (weak / far)
A rough reference — real values vary by device and environment.

05 Distance estimation — and why it’s approximate

The distance shown is an educated guess from signal strength, not a measurement.

GHOSTTOOTH estimates distance from RSSI using a path-loss model (signal fades in a predictable way as it travels). It’s a helpful rough guide — think “hotter / colder,” not a tape measure.

Why it’s only approximate — things that skew it:

  • Walls, furniture and metal absorb or block the signal, making devices look farther than they are.
  • Human bodies (including yours) are mostly water and dampen 2.4 GHz signals — turning around can change the reading.
  • Reflections / multipath — signals bounce off walls and floors and arrive by several paths at once.
  • Transmit power differs between devices; a low-power tag and a high-power beacon at the same distance read very differently.
  • Phone chipset differences — every phone’s radio calibrates RSSI a little differently.
  • Orientation of both the device and your phone (antennas are directional).
  • Interference from Wi-Fi, other Bluetooth, microwaves and crowds sharing the 2.4 GHz band.

Bottom line: use distance as a trend. Watching how it changes as you move is far more reliable than any single number.

06 Why we can’t detect 100% of devices

No Bluetooth scanner — on any phone, from anyone — can guarantee it will find every device. GHOSTTOOTH shows you a lot, but here’s exactly where the ceiling is:

  • Off, asleep, or silent. A device that isn’t currently advertising is invisible. Many devices sleep to save battery and only “wake” occasionally, or stop advertising once paired to something.
  • Classic Bluetooth vs BLE. GHOSTTOOTH focuses on BLE advertisements. Older Classic Bluetooth (BR/EDR) devices that aren’t in discoverable mode may not appear.
  • Randomized / rotating MAC addresses. For privacy, many devices change their Bluetooth address every few minutes. The same tracker can therefore show up as several different entries, and can’t always be re-identified across time.
  • Non-Bluetooth trackers are invisible. A bug that reports over GPS, cellular (SIM), or Wi-Fi only emits no Bluetooth signal — a Bluetooth scanner simply cannot see it.
  • Operating-system limits. Background-scan throttling, Doze/battery optimisation, and per-app quotas can reduce how many packets your phone reports.
  • Encrypted / opaque payloads. Some advertisements are deliberately obfuscated, so little can be read beyond “something is here.”

Treat a clean scan as “nothing obvious right now,” not “definitely nothing.” GHOSTTOOTH is an awareness aid, not a guarantee.

07 Manufacturer codes / Company Identifiers

A number inside the packet that often (but not always) reveals the maker.

Advertisement packets can carry manufacturer-specific data that begins with a Company Identifier — a number assigned by the Bluetooth SIG to a specific vendor. GHOSTTOOTH maps that number to a company name wherever it can (from an embedded registry of thousands of vendors).

  • “Unknown” is normal. Not every device includes a Company Identifier, many chips are generic silicon vendors shared by thousands of unrelated products, and the registry can’t cover everything. Seeing Unknown is common and not suspicious by itself.
  • A manufacturer ID is not proof of ownership or identity. IDs can be reused across many product lines, or spoofed. “Made by Company X” doesn’t mean “operated by Company X,” and a familiar brand name doesn’t confirm what the device actually is.

Use the manufacturer as one clue among several (name, services, signal behaviour), never as a verdict.

09 How to physically locate a suspicious device

Play “hot and cold” with the signal.

  1. Pick your target row and keep an eye on its RSSI and estimated distance.
  2. Walk slowly around the space. Move a few steps, pause, and watch the numbers settle.
  3. Follow the heat. As RSSI climbs toward 0 (e.g. −80 → −55 → −40) and the estimated distance shrinks, you’re getting closer. If it drops, you’re moving away.
  4. Narrow it down. Sweep high and low, and check enclosed spots — bags, seats, vents, furniture, vehicle interiors.
  5. Expect wobble. Readings jump due to reflections and obstacles; trust the overall trend over several readings, not one spike.
Your privacy

10 Privacy

GHOSTTOOTH scans locally, on your device. The Bluetooth advertisements it hears are processed on your phone to build the list you see.

  • No accounts, no ads, no third-party tracking SDKs.
  • Scan data is not sold or shared, and by default the device information you capture stays on your phone.
  • Any network use is limited to fetching the app’s public reference data (like the manufacturer registry) — never your scan results.

In short: it’s a listener, not a reporter. For the exact, authoritative details, see the app’s in-store privacy policy.

11 Legal & ethical use

GHOSTTOOTH is provided for personal safety, education, and awareness. By using it you agree to use it responsibly:

  • Obey local laws. Rules on radio monitoring, recording, and privacy differ by country and region — you are responsible for complying with yours.
  • Don’t misuse it. Never use GHOSTTOOTH to stalk, harass, surveil, or track another person, or to interfere with devices you don’t own.
  • It is not a certified security or forensics tool. Results are best-effort and probabilistic; do not rely on it as sole evidence or as a guarantee of safety.
  • If you fear for your safety, contact local authorities or a professional sweep service.

12 Troubleshooting FAQ

Quick answers to the most common questions.

I see no devices at all
  • Make sure Bluetooth is ON and the app has the Nearby devices (or Location, on older Android) permission granted.
  • Press Start scan and wait 10–30 seconds — some devices advertise infrequently.
  • Move to where devices are likely present; a truly empty radio environment is possible but rare.
  • Toggle Bluetooth off and on, or restart the app, to reset the radio.
Everything shows “Unknown”

That’s normal. Many devices don’t include a Company Identifier, use a generic chipset vendor, or aren’t in the registry. Absence of a name isn’t suspicious — judge devices by their overall behaviour (signal, services, persistence), not the label alone.

The distances jump around

Expected. Distance is estimated from a noisy signal that bounces off walls, bodies and metal. Don’t read a single value — watch the trend as you move (section 05 & 09).

Why does the same tracker show up twice (or many times)?

Privacy features. Many devices rotate their MAC address every few minutes, so one physical device can appear as several entries over time. It’s not a bug — it’s the device protecting against long-term tracking.

Why do I need location permission for a Bluetooth app?

On older Android versions the OS ties BLE scanning to the Location permission, because beacons can reveal location. GHOSTTOOTH doesn’t use GPS or track you (see section 03). On Android 12+ it declares its scan as neverForLocation.

A device disappeared from the list

It likely stopped advertising, went to sleep, moved out of range, or rotated to a new address. Rescan and move around; if it’s a rotating device, it may reappear under a different entry.

Can GHOSTTOOTH find hidden cameras or bugs that don’t use Bluetooth?

No. If a device communicates only over Wi-Fi, cellular, or GPS — or stores footage locally with no radio — it emits no Bluetooth signal and cannot be detected here. See section 06.

13 Glossary

The jargon, decoded.

BLE (Bluetooth Low Energy)
A low-power flavour of Bluetooth used by trackers, wearables and sensors. It’s the technology GHOSTTOOTH primarily listens to.
Advertisement packet
The small, public broadcast a BLE device sends to announce its presence. GHOSTTOOTH reads these without connecting.
RSSI
Received Signal Strength Indicator — how strongly your phone hears a device, reported in dBm.
dBm
Decibel-milliwatts, the unit for RSSI. Values are negative; closer to 0 means a stronger signal.
MAC address
A device’s Bluetooth hardware address. Often randomized/rotating for privacy, so it may change over time.
Company Identifier
A number assigned by the Bluetooth SIG to a vendor, embedded in manufacturer data. Maps to a maker — but can be reused or spoofed.
Service UUID
An identifier for a Bluetooth “service” (a capability) that a device advertises, hinting at what it does.
GATT
Generic Attribute Profile — the structure BLE uses to exchange data after connecting. GHOSTTOOTH stays passive and does not connect.
Path loss
The predictable way a radio signal weakens with distance and obstacles — the basis for turning RSSI into an estimated distance.
Bluetooth SIG
The Special Interest Group that maintains Bluetooth standards and assigns Company Identifiers.
